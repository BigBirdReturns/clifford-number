# The Self-Assembling Map

## What this is for

The goal is not a UK-AI-policy research artifact that one person hand-curates. The
goal is a **map of the plausible-deniability layer** — the surfaces through which
actors, capital, policy machinery, and outcomes move — that **assembles itself**
from public records across every industry and nation, so that the human role
collapses from *authoring* to *pruning*.

This document is the architecture for that inversion. It supersedes the labor
model assumed in `docs/durability-plan.md` (which analyzed how to preserve a
frozen hand-curated artifact) without discarding that document's evidence and
removal protocols — those become *more* important at scale, not less.

The single design tension, stated once: at "every industry and nation" scale, the
only thing separating a civic-infrastructure map from an indiscriminate dossier on
named individuals is the guardrail discipline. So the guardrails are not policy
bolted on top — they are load-bearing architecture, enforced by code at every
stage. Every automation decision below is constrained by them.

## The inversion

**Today:**

```
human reads a source → human authors surface + participation + receipt rows → compiler
```

**Target:**

```
connector sweeps a public registry
  → observation store (raw normalized facts, append-only)
  → entity resolution (cluster name strings to canonical actors/orgs)
  → surface synthesizer (rules turn resolved observations into candidate surfaces)
  → PRUNE QUEUE (ranked machine proposals)
  → human prunes: accept / reject / merge / split / downgrade
  → verified ledger (human-accepted rows only)
  → compiler → app
```

The human never types a row from a blank line again. They judge machine proposals.
Every rejection is a training signal that suppresses similar future proposals. The
machine does the generation; a small number of pruners can maintain an
arbitrarily large map. **This is also the answer to the bus-factor-1 problem** from
the durability plan: authoring does not scale to one person; pruning does, and it
parallelizes across volunteers who need judgment, not the whole methodology in
their head.

## The layers

### 1. Connectors — the fuel intake `[partly exists]`

One connector per public registry. Each sweeps its source and normalizes every
row into a common **observation** record. The existing `tools/research/`
connectors are the prototypes; they need two changes: emit observations to a
store instead of ad-hoc CSVs, and generalize from hardcoded target lists
(`['Palantir','SpaceX',...]`) to *sweep-by-registry* so the map grows without
someone naming targets in advance.

The fuel is public-role registries, and there are more than enough to cover most
of the developed world:

| Class | Sources (examples) |
| --- | --- |
| Lobbying | US Senate LDA `[exists]`, EU Transparency Register, UK APPC/ORCL |
| Corporate | SEC EDGAR `[exists]`, UK Companies House, EU BRIS, OpenCorporates |
| Procurement | USASpending `[exists]`, UK Find-a-Tender, EU TED, world-bank tenders |
| Nonprofit | IRS 990/TEOS `[exists]`, UK Charity Commission, EU equivalents |
| Public office | gazettes, appointment registers, register-of-interests filings |
| Research/IP | grant databases, patent assignees, standards-body rosters |

A **registry catalog** (`data/registries/*.json`) lists each source per
jurisdiction and sector, its access method, cadence, and which entity/surface
types it yields. This is how "every nation" becomes tractable: contributors
onboard a *registry once*, and its connector sweeps it forever. You scale by
adding registries, not rows. The map is only ever as good as the public
registries a jurisdiction actually maintains — a real and honest ceiling in
places with weak transparency law.

### 2. Observation store — raw intake, not the ledger `[new]`

`data/observations/*.jsonl`: append-only, normalized facts straight from
connectors. Each observation carries source URL, retrieval date, and content
hash. This is disposable and fully re-derivable from the connectors — it is
**not** the durable asset and it is **not** published. It is the machine's
scratch space between the registries and the ledger. Keeping it separate is what
lets the verified ledger stay small, clean, and citable while the intake churns.

### 3. Entity resolution — the make-or-break core `[new, hardest]`

Cluster observation name-strings to canonical actors and organizations.
`PALANTIR TECHNOLOGIES` in a contract award, `Palantir Technologies Inc.` in a
Form D, and `Palantir` in a lobbying filing must resolve to one node.

This is the layer that decides whether self-assembly works, and it carries the
project's sharpest risk: **a false merge of two people is a defamatory factual
error generated by a machine.** So the rule is asymmetric — the synthesizer may
auto-merge organizations above a high-confidence threshold, but **any merge that
attaches a claim to a named individual requires human confirmation** in the prune
queue, never silent auto-merge. Confidence is recorded, not assumed. This single
constraint is what keeps automated breadth from becoming automated libel.

### 4. Surface synthesizer — generation as rules `[new]`

The rule engine that turns resolved observations into **candidate bounded
surfaces**, encoding the project's existing surface-type taxonomy as generators.
Examples:

- two resolved actors are officers of the same company in an overlapping period
  → *founder/officer surface* candidate (hop-eligible);
- an org appears as awardee on a procurement record → *customer/vendor surface*
  candidate (scorable, hop-eligibility depends on actor co-participation);
- an actor named to a public advisory body → *government advisory surface*
  candidate.

Each candidate is emitted pre-filled: `surface_type`, participants, the
observations as receipts, and a machine `confidence` plus `evidence_class`. The
synthesizer **never** promotes a broad institution to a hop (the core rule from
`README.md`), and the redaction `BANNED`-field gate runs here a second time, on
every candidate, in code.

### 5. Prune queue — the human workspace `[new, extends scout + import-queue]`

The one place humans work. Machine candidates ranked by confidence × impact.
Actions: **accept, reject (with a reason code), merge, split, downgrade evidence,
mark unverifiable.** Rejections feed back as suppression rules so the same bad
proposal does not return — otherwise pruning just becomes the new authoring
bottleneck with extra steps. Ranking and aggressive auto-suppression of
low-confidence noise are what keep the queue humane; if a pruner sees mostly
garbage, the confidence model is miscalibrated and that is the bug to fix.

The prune decisions are themselves **durable data** (`data/decisions/*.jsonl`) —
they are the irreplaceable human judgment, the one thing in the whole pipeline a
future regeneration *cannot* recompute. Treat them with the same preservation
weight as the ledger.

### 6. Verified ledger — the durable core `[exists]`

Only human-accepted rows land here. This stays exactly what
`docs/durability-plan.md` protects: plain-text, receipt-backed, citable,
mirror-able, tombstone-removable. **Two-tier provenance is absolute:**
`machine_proposed` never appears in the published map; only `human_verified` does.
The public artifact shows verified topology; the proposal churn stays behind the
prune gate. This is the firewall that lets the map be aggressive about *intake*
and conservative about *assertion* at the same time.

### 7. Compiler + app — unchanged `[exists]`

Rebuild hops, scores, and the static site from the verified ledger. No change to
the compiler law: the app computes the number only from `build/hop-graph.json`,
generated from verified participation in bounded surfaces.

## Guardrails, as enforced code (not aspirations)

1. **Public-role-only by construction.** Connectors ingest only public-role
   registries. There is no path in the architecture that scrapes private data;
   the redaction `BANNED` list runs at intake *and* at synthesis.
2. **Verified/proposed firewall.** Machine confidence never becomes published
   fact without a human accept. The public map is provably a subset of
   human-verified rows.
3. **No machine claims about individuals without confirmation.** Person-level
   merges and person-level surfaces above the confidence floor always route to a
   human. Defamation risk scales with named individuals; this is where it is
   contained.
4. **The listing caveat travels with every row.** A registry appearance proves an
   appearance, not attendance, membership, endorsement, guilt, or wrongdoing —
   generated rows carry this exactly as hand-authored ones do.
5. **Removal protocol applies to generated rows too.** Tombstones, severability,
   and the history-rewrite procedure from the durability plan govern how
   auto-generated content about a person honestly leaves the map.

## Durability of a living system

A self-assembling map's durable assets are different from a frozen artifact's:

- **Regenerable:** connectors, synthesis rules, observation store, the compiler,
  the app. If the engine rots (Node, the toolchain, an API), it is rebuilt from
  the registry catalog + the format spec, and re-verified against committed
  `build/` outputs. The assembly is *reproducible*, so the engine is disposable.
- **Irreplaceable:** the verified ledger + receipts (as before) **and** the prune
  decision log (§5). Automation can regenerate candidates forever; it can never
  regenerate the human accept/reject judgments. Back those up like the ledger.

So the durability plan's five protocols (archive-on-ingest, format spec, mirrors +
Software Heritage + Zenodo, removal/tombstone, succession) all still apply —
plus one: **the decision log is a first-class durable artifact.**

## Honest limits

- **Entity resolution is genuinely hard and gates everything.** Cross-jurisdiction
  identity matching is an unsolved-in-general problem; the plan mitigates with
  confidence thresholds and mandatory human confirmation for person-level merges,
  not with a claim that it is easy.
- **"Every industry and nation" is unbounded; the realistic path is
  registry-by-registry.** Breadth is paced by transparency law that varies wildly
  by country. The map will be dense where public records are strong and thin where
  they are not, and it should say so rather than imply uniform coverage.
- **Pruning can re-become a bottleneck.** If confidence ranking is poor, humans
  drown. Calibration of the confidence model is the ongoing engineering, not a
  one-time build.
- **Legal exposure scales with breadth and with named individuals.** The
  verified/proposed firewall and public-role-only rule are the mitigations; they
  must hold under automation, which is why they are code, not documentation.

## Build order

1. **Observation store + one refactored connector.** Point an existing
   `tools/research/` connector at `data/observations/` with hashes and dates.
   Smallest end-to-end proof.
2. **Entity resolution v1** — organizations only, high-confidence auto-merge,
   everything else queued. Prove the hard layer on the safe half first.
3. **Surface synthesizer v1** — one or two rules (officer-overlap,
   procurement-awardee) emitting candidates with confidence.
4. **Prune queue** — extend the scout report / import queue into an accept/reject
   workspace with reason codes and a decision log.
5. **Person-level resolution + firewall hardening** — only after 1–4 are solid,
   and only behind mandatory human confirmation.
6. **Registry catalog + second jurisdiction** — prove "another nation" by
   onboarding one non-US/UK registry through the same pipeline.

Each step is independently useful and independently safe. Nothing about a named
person is auto-published before step 5, by construction.
